# Who Actually Buys? Finding the Real Economic Buyer in Enterprise Deals

The economic buyer is the person who controls budget and holds veto power over an enterprise deal, and in most large organizations that person is not the one with the most impressive title in your CRM. Gartner research on complex B2B purchases finds that a typical buying group now includes [6 to 10 decision makers](https://intentamplify.com/blog/b2b-buying-decision-process/), each doing independent research before a seller ever gets a call back. If you are pitching the VP whose name is on the RFP, you may be pitching an influencer, not the buyer. Founders who close enterprise deals faster are the ones who map the committee before they build the deck.

## What Is an Economic Buyer, and Why Isn't It Always Obvious?

The term "economic buyer" comes from enterprise sales methodology, not marketing. Miller Heiman's Strategic Selling framework, one of the earliest formal models for enterprise sales, defines four distinct buying roles in every deal: the Economic Buyer, the User Buyer, the Technical Buyer, and the Coach, and it explicitly warns that [job titles often do not reveal the actual role a person plays](https://salesmotion.io/blog/miller-heimann) in a purchase. A VP of Engineering might be the technical buyer who blocks a deal on compliance grounds, while the actual economic buyer sits two levels up in finance or operations and never joins a demo call.

MEDDIC, the qualification framework still taught in enterprise sales orgs today, builds this distinction into its name. It was [created inside PTC in 1996 by Dick Dunkel, working under SVP John McMahon alongside Jack Napoli](https://meddicc.com/resources/who-created-meddic), and the "E" stands specifically for Economic Buyer, separate from champions, technical evaluators, and end users. Three decades later, the framework has outlived the software category it was built for because the underlying problem, misidentifying who actually approves the spend, has not gone away.

## How Big Is a Typical Enterprise Buying Committee?

The committee has grown, not shrunk. Gartner's research on B2B purchasing puts the average buying group for complex solutions at [six to ten stakeholders](https://intentamplify.com/blog/b2b-buying-decision-process/), spanning procurement, finance, legal, security, and multiple business units. That is not a single "decision-maker" you can find on LinkedIn and cold-email your way to. It is a small internal negotiation you are not in the room for, and your champion is translating your pitch to the rest of that group whether you know it or not.

This matters more than it used to. In a 2024 survey of 632 B2B buyers, Gartner found that [74 percent of buying teams experience unhealthy conflict during the decision process](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-05-07-gartner-sales-survey-finds-74-percent-of-b2b-buyer-teams-demonstrate-unhealthy-conflict-during-the-decision-process), meaning members disagree on the right course of action or get overruled by outside decision-makers. The same research found that buying groups who reach consensus are 2.5 times more likely to report a high-quality deal outcome. If you cannot identify who has final authority in that group, you cannot help them reach that consensus, and the deal drags.

## Mapping the Committee: Roles You Need to Identify

Before you can find the economic buyer, separate the committee into roles, not titles:

- **Economic buyer.** Controls the budget line and can kill or approve the deal unilaterally, regardless of what anyone else on the committee wants.
- **Technical evaluator.** Assesses whether the product meets requirements: security, integration, compliance, architecture fit. Can block a deal but rarely approves one alone.
- **User buyer.** Will use the product day to day. Cares about workflow and adoption, has influence but usually not budget authority.
- **Coach or champion.** Internal advocate who wants you to win and will tell you things the economic buyer never will, including who the economic buyer actually is.

This mapping is not academic. It is the fastest way to stop spending cycles pitching the wrong person the wrong argument.

## Budget Control vs. Influence: The Signals That Tell Them Apart

Titles lie. Behavior does not. A few reliable signals separate the person who controls budget from the person who merely influences the process:

**Who can say yes alone, and who needs to check.** In every enterprise deal, someone eventually says "let me confirm with my boss" or "I need this signed off." That confirmation loop points directly at the economic buyer, even if that person never appears on a call.

**Who owns the line item, not the use case.** The technical evaluator cares whether the product works. The economic buyer cares whether the spend is justified against a budget they are accountable for. Ask where the budget lives organizationally, not just who requested the RFP.

**Who shows up only at the end.** Economic buyers are often invisible during early evaluation and appear only for final approval or a pricing conversation. Their absence from the weeds is itself a signal, not a lack of importance.

**Who can override a "no" from the rest of the room.** If a champion tells you the deal is dead but someone else can revive it with a single conversation, that person, not the champion, is the real buyer.

**How procurement engages.** When legal, security, or procurement enters the process late but with unusual authority to change terms, that function is often acting as a proxy for the economic buyer's risk tolerance. This is consistent with Gartner's finding that unhealthy conflict frequently stems from external decision-makers overruling the buying team.

## Adjusting Your Pitch for Economic Buyers vs. Technical Evaluators

The same product needs two different pitches, because these two audiences are optimizing for different outcomes.

**For the economic buyer**, lead with the cost of inaction and the return on the specific budget line they own. They do not want a feature tour. They want to know what breaks if they do nothing, what the payback period looks like, and how this compares to the next best alternative use of that budget. Keep the technical detail minimal and put the business case first.

**For the technical evaluator**, lead with architecture, integration effort, security posture, and how the solution fails gracefully. This audience is graded internally on risk they introduce, not revenue they generate, so their incentive is to find reasons to say no. Give them the detail that lets them defend a "yes" to their own leadership.

A common mistake is pitching the economic buyer like a technical evaluator, burying the business case in implementation detail, or pitching the technical evaluator like an economic buyer, which reads as evasive and light on substance. Match the pitch to the role, not to whoever is in the room.

## Why the Buyer Is Non-Obvious: Patterns Across Industries

The economic buyer's identity shifts by industry, and founders selling across verticals need different mental models for each:

**Healthcare and health systems.** The clinical champion who wants your product is rarely the economic buyer. Purchasing routes through value analysis committees and finance, where budget decisions weigh clinical benefit against system-wide cost, often on a timeline the clinical champion cannot control.

**Financial services and banks.** A business unit head may want your product urgently, but risk, compliance, and often a central procurement function hold effective veto power, and budget frequently sits with a shared technology or operations budget rather than the requesting unit.

**Manufacturing and industrial.** Plant-level operations staff can be enthusiastic users, but capital budget for new tools frequently sits with corporate finance or a regional VP who has never seen the shop floor and evaluates the purchase purely against capex approval thresholds.

**Public sector and education.** A department head can champion a tool for months, but budget authority sits with a business office, board, or appropriations cycle that operates on a calendar the champion does not control, which is why public-sector deals often stall right when they seem closest to done.

These are patterns, not rules, and every organization has exceptions. The discipline is to test the pattern against the specific account rather than assume it.

## The Real Cost of Getting This Wrong

Misidentifying the economic buyer does not just slow a deal down, it produces false positives that waste a sales cycle. A champion who loves the product, a technical evaluator who signs off, and a warm final call can all happen and the deal can still die, because none of those people could actually authorize the spend. Gartner's research on buying group conflict points at exactly this failure mode: when a seller does not understand the internal dynamics of the committee, they cannot help the group reach the consensus that predicts a high-quality outcome.

For founders raising the stakes of every enterprise deal against a finite sales team, this is not a minor efficiency gain. It is the difference between a pipeline of deals that convert and a pipeline of deals that stall indefinitely at "let me check internally."

## A Practical Framework for Founders

Before your next enterprise pitch, run this check:

1. Name the budget owner, not the requester, for this specific purchase.
2. Ask your champion directly who has to approve spend of this size, and who has killed similar deals before.
3. Watch for the confirmation loop, whoever says "let me check" is pointing you toward the real buyer.
4. Build two versions of your pitch: one built around business impact for the economic buyer, one built around technical risk for the evaluator.
5. Treat procurement and legal engagement as a signal of the economic buyer's risk tolerance, not just a formality to survive.
