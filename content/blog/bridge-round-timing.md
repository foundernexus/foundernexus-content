TL;DR: a bridge round is either buying time to hit one specific, nameable milestone, or it's delaying a harder call about whether the business works. The whole decision turns on which one is true, and most founders reach for the bridge before they've been honest about that.

The decision underneath "should I raise a bridge" is not "can I get the money." It's whether the next six to nine months of runway change the story a real investor will fund, or just postpone the moment you have to face that the story hasn't changed. Naming which of those you're actually in is the entire job here. Everything else is mechanics.

The reason this is hard is that both situations feel identical from the inside. You're low on runway. The priced round you wanted isn't there yet. A bridge from existing investors or an angel feels like the obvious way to keep going. Sometimes it is exactly right. Just as often it's the thing that turns a hard twelve-month problem into a brutal twenty-month one.

## Decision one: what specific thing does the bridge buy?

The healthy version of a bridge has a nameable target. A pricing change you need two more quarters to prove. A product milestone that unlocks a category of customer. A key hire whose ramp closes the gap the last round left open. Something concrete that, once true, makes the next round a different conversation.

<div class="pull">A bridge is only a bridge if you can name the far side of it. If the honest answer to "what does this money let you prove" is "more of the same, for longer," you're not building a bridge. You're renting time.</div>

The test is uncomfortable but simple. Finish this sentence out loud: "This money lets us prove ______, and once we've proven it, the round we couldn't raise becomes raiseable because ______." If you can fill both blanks with something specific, you likely have a real bridge. If you find yourself reaching for generic momentum language, that's the signal the milestone isn't there yet.

## Decision two: is the gap timing, or is it the business?

Some companies are genuinely a quarter or two of proof away from a round the market will fund. Others have hit a ceiling the founder hasn't fully admitted: the motion doesn't repeat, the market is smaller than the deck claims, the retention doesn't hold. A bridge does nothing for the second case except delay the reckoning and burn goodwill with the people funding it.

The way founders who navigate this well tell the difference is by looking at what stalled the primary round. If investors passed on execution risk, and the bridge closes that specific risk, timing is the real issue. If investors passed on the fundamental shape of the business, no amount of runway changes their answer. You'd be paying to ask the same question again later, from a weaker position.

<div class="pull">Ask why the round you wanted didn't happen. If the answer is "we needed one more proof point," a bridge can work. If the answer is "they didn't believe the business," a bridge just buys you the same no, at a higher burn.</div>

## Decision three: what does the bridge cost beyond the money?

A bridge is rarely free in the way it looks. It usually comes with terms that compound: discounts and caps that stack against your next priced round, a signal to the market about where you are, and the quiet cost of your existing investors now holding more risk in a company they're watching more nervously. None of that is a reason to avoid a bridge. It's a reason to price the real cost before you take it.

The founders who get burned treat the bridge as neutral runway and discover the terms reshaped their next round. The ones who navigate it well go in clear-eyed about the signal they're sending and the position the terms leave them in if the milestone slips. They also ask the harder question: if this money doesn't get me to the milestone, what then? A bridge with no honest answer to "and if it doesn't work" is a bridge to nowhere.

<div class="checkbox">
<h3>Before you take the bridge, sit with these</h3>
<ul>
<li>Can I name the specific milestone this money lets me prove?</li>
<li>Once that milestone is true, does the round I couldn't raise become raiseable, and why?</li>
<li>Did the primary round stall on execution risk the bridge closes, or on belief in the business?</li>
<li>What do the terms cost me in my next priced round if the milestone slips?</li>
<li>If this money doesn't get me there, what's the plan then, and have I actually said it out loud?</li>
</ul>
</div>

## The pattern underneath all three

The founders who use a bridge well treat it as a deliberate bet on a named milestone, with the cost priced and the downside faced. They know exactly what they're buying and what happens if the bet misses. The ones who struggle reach for the bridge as relief from a hard conversation, raise it on vague momentum, and find themselves twelve months later at the same wall with less runway and worse terms.

The bridge is a tool. Whether it's the right one depends entirely on which situation you're actually in, and that's the part that's hardest to see clearly from inside your own company.
