TL;DR: of everything out there, Hampton is the community FounderNexus gets compared to most. Both are invite-only, both are built for founders, both take curation seriously. Here's the honest read on where they actually part ways.

## Where Hampton is genuinely strong

Hampton is genuinely well-built, and for a lot of founders it's the right call. Here's the honest case for it, made properly.

<ul class="clean">
<li><strong>Excellent curation and breadth.</strong> Hampton vets carefully and spans founders, CEOs, and executives across many kinds of companies, including large bootstrapped and lifestyle businesses, not only venture-backed ones. That range is real value if you want it.</li>
<li><strong>Core groups plus a wide network.</strong> The eight-person peer core group is paired with a large surrounding community, digital tools, and events, so you get both intimacy and reach.</li>
<li><strong>Strong on the whole founder.</strong> Wealth, health, personal life, and business all sit inside the Hampton conversation, not just company decisions.</li>
<li><strong>Polished experience.</strong> The product, events, and member experience are well-run and well-resourced.</li>
</ul>

<div class="callout fair">
<h3>If this describes you, Hampton is probably the right room</h3>
<p>You want a carefully curated founder community that spans venture and non-venture companies. You value the whole-founder conversation, not just the business one. You want both a tight core group and a broad network around it. If that's you, Hampton does it well, and for a lot of founders it's simply the better fit. None of this is a knock on Hampton.</p>
</div>

## Where FounderNexus is built differently

The real difference is focus. Hampton is a broad, excellent founder community. FounderNexus is deliberately narrower: venture-scale founders only, organized by stage, convened around the specific decision in front of them.

<ul class="clean">
<li><strong>Venture-scale specificity.</strong> Hampton intentionally includes many company types. FounderNexus is only venture-backed founders, so every peer shares the same board, dilution, and growth context.</li>
<li><strong>Stage as the organizing filter.</strong> Hampton groups by curation and fit broadly. FounderNexus clusters by company stage first, so the room is founders carrying the same class of decision at the same moment.</li>
<li><strong>The room forms around the decision.</strong> Hampton's core group is a standing peer group. FounderNexus convenes a room around the live call, so the composition matches the decision, not just the calendar.</li>
<li><strong>Decision quality over whole-life breadth.</strong> Both are high-signal. Hampton leans wider, whole founder, whole life. FounderNexus leans deeper on the venture decision itself.</li>
</ul>

## How to decide

This is a genuinely close call, and honestly both can be right at different points in your life. If you want breadth, a polished whole-founder community, and peers across many kinds of companies including non-venture ones, Hampton is an excellent choice, and may well be the better one for you.

But if you're specifically venture-scale, and your sharpest need is a room of founders at your exact stage pressure-testing the exact decision you're carrying, that narrowness is the entire point of FounderNexus. It's what we optimize for, on purpose, over breadth.
