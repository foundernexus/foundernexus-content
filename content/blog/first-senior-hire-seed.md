TL;DR: the first real executive hire is one of the most consequential calls a seed founder makes, and one of the most deceptively reversible-looking. It looks like a hiring decision. It's really a decision about what the company needs to become next.

Almost every seed founder makes this hire under the same pressure. You're doing three jobs. One of them is clearly slipping. Bringing in a senior person feels like the obvious fix. Sometimes it is. Just as often, the timing or the seat or the profile is wrong in a way you can't see until six months and one painful exit later.

This guide is about the decisions underneath the hire, the ones that actually determine whether it works. Not the interviewing tactics you can find anywhere else.

## Decision one: is the seat actually real yet?

The most expensive version of this hire is bringing in a senior person to own a function that doesn't really exist yet. A VP of Sales with no repeatable motion to scale. A Head of Product with no product-market signal to build on. They show up expecting a machine to optimize and find a blank page instead. Building from a blank page is founder work, not executive work.

<div class="pull">A senior hire scales something that already works. If the thing doesn't work yet, you're not hiring an executive at all. You're outsourcing a decision you still have to make yourself.</div>

The honest test is this. Can you describe, specifically, what "working" looks like in this function today? And is the job to scale that, or to invent it from nothing? If it's to invent it, the seat isn't real yet, and the hire is early.

## Decision two: builder, or manager in builder's clothing?

At seed, you need someone who can operate with almost no scaffolding. No team under them. No process. No clean data. Someone who can build the function from close to zero. That is a genuinely different person from the one who runs a mature function well.

The failure mode is hiring an impressive operator from a much larger company, someone who ran a function but never built one. Their instincts are tuned for optimizing and delegating. Your open job requires doing and inventing. The resume reads like a level-up. The reality is a mismatch.

<ul class="clean">
<li><strong>Signals of a builder.</strong> They talk about the earliest, messiest version of things they made. They ask what's broken, not what's the headcount. They're energized, not alarmed, by the absence of process.</li>
<li><strong>Signals of a manager in builder's clothing.</strong> Their best stories are about scale, not zero-to-one. They ask about resources and reports early. Ambiguity reads to them as disorganization rather than opportunity.</li>
</ul>

## Decision three: can you afford to be wrong?

At seed, a bad senior hire is never just a salary line. It's six months of your attention. It's a chunk of runway. It's the right hire you didn't make. It's the morale hit when it unwinds. So the question isn't only "is this the right person." It's "what's the blast radius if I'm wrong, and can the company absorb it right now?"

Founders who navigate this well often stage the commitment. A well-scoped contract. A fractional arrangement. A tightly defined first project before the full-time offer. Not because they're indecisive, but because they're pricing the downside honestly.

<div class="checkbox">
<h3>Before you make the offer, sit with these</h3>
<ul>
<li>Can I describe what "working" looks like in this function today?</li>
<li>Is the job to scale that, or to invent it from near zero?</li>
<li>Has this person built a function from scratch, or only run a mature one?</li>
<li>What's the blast radius if this is wrong, and can the company absorb it now?</li>
<li>Is there a lower-risk way to test this before the full commitment?</li>
</ul>
</div>

## The pattern underneath all three

The founders who get this right treat the first senior hire as a decision about the company's next chapter, not a staffing gap to plug. They're clear about what's already real versus what's still theirs to invent. They're honest about the gap between building and managing. They price the downside deliberately. The ones who struggle treat the hire as relief, reach for the impressive resume, and find the mismatch months later.
